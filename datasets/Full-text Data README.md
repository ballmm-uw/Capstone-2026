*Although this project has focused primarily on Named Entity Recognition, programmatic validation of information, and crowdsourcing, one of the surprising and exciting results was the production of a .csv file that contains the full text data for all of the oral histories our team has been working with. Below is a short narrative that details its creation, explains its contents, and demonstrates potential uses for the dataset.*
 
## A Note on the Full-text Spreadsheet of the AIP Oral History Transcripts
 
At the beginning of this project our team was supplied with a large JSON file containing the complete raw data relating to all of the oral histories held by the American Institute of Physics' Niels Bohr Library and Archives. The intention of our project was to experiment with applying artificial intelligence and natural language processing tools to this collection in order to imagine new approaches to search, navigation, and description. Through the course of the project our team worked with and manipulated this data in many different ways, and although the primary focus of the project was elsewhere, a serendipitous result was a single spreadsheet that contains the full-text and all associated metadata with these histories. The formatting of this data as such, is extremely powerful when used in tandem with the programming language R and application R Studio, presenting many exciting opportunities for digital scholarship related to this collection.
 
The AIP oral history collection consists of over 2000 oral histories dating from 1959 to current day and contains interviews with some of the most prominent and well-known physical scientists from the 20th and 21st centuries. While the audio recordings are available upon request from AIP, the text transcripts are freely accessible online and available to be downloaded individually in several file formats like .pdf, .txt, and .png. Despite this, the initial provision of a complete dataset was extremely valuable and crucial to our beginning efforts.
 
We began by parsing the raw dataset back into individual transcripts so we could run smaller and limited programmatic experiments. The raw data was made up of HTML strings inside of one large JSON file, suggesting that this raw data had likely been exported directly from the AIP Content Management System. Our team decided to keep this format when splitting the raw data into individual JSONs for each different oral history as it did not affect our attempts to run Named Entity Recognition scripts on the files. Notably, we consistently ran into an error with our parsing script at interview number 1576. This was due to a formatting issue we could not immediately resolve or identify due to the size of the raw data. As such, the decision was made to proceed with an incomplete corpus knowing that any code and pipelines that were established could be applied to a full dataset later on. Our current theory is that character data from oral histories in other languages, mainly German, might be leading to this error.
 
As we began to develop workflows to process these transcripts our work gradually began to focus more on the validation of metadata, and then eventually methods of programmatically identifying subjects in need of additional or potentially reparative public description. As our final deliverable took the shape of an application that could be used to crowdsource oral history subjects that lacked Wikipedia pages, an exciting opportunity was recognized when setting up the backend database for our web app. Using the open-source databases management service Supabase, our raw JSONs were formatted into tables upon their upload, and became exportable as .csv files as the service operates on PostgreSQL. From concurrent coursework in Data Science, our team noted the potential for working with this in this format using the programming language R, and the full-text data was downloaded to be curated and handed off as an additional deliverable in this project.
 
One final step was taken for cleaning the data, and a python script (using Pandas and the BeautifulSoup libraries) was used to strip the HTML elements from the spreadsheet, leaving only text values behind. This was a new experience for us, and as relatively inexperienced programmers it is important to note that the original unprocessed data (still in HTML format) along with the processing script, will be handed off to AIP for the sake of provenance and reproducibility. While the BeautifulSoup library is a widely used python library for this task, a known problem is often the accidental removal of URLs from datasets. Although steps were taken to mitigate this, a more experienced programmer/researcher may want to revisit this cleaning process to ensure that external links within the dataset were not lost.
 
Once stripped of the HTML, each row of text represents an oral history interview, with the columns holding the full text of the interview along with values from the original metadata schema. In the interest of discretion and brevity not every field will be detailed here, however there are a total of 24 fields. Several of the more significant ones are:
 
- Abstract - Full text of the abstract written by AIP
- Additional Persons - Names of additional people mentioned in the interview, identified by AIP catalogers and librarians
- Institutions - Names of institutions mentioned in the interview
- Interview date
- Interviewer
- Interviewee imagine - Links to AIP Visual Archives
- Interview location
- Preferred citation
- Related audio
- Related images
- Field subjects - Indexing terms selected by AIP catalogers and librarians
- Body - The full text of the interview

With the access that the spreadsheet format affords, it's critical to consider the personal nature of this data and keep in mind ethical practices when engaging with it. As is the case with many digital humanities projects, by working with information that might not have been originally designed to be interacted with digitally, and by making this available and more readily available via the internet, the increased visibility also carries more possibilities for the harmful use of this information. Even acts that are not intentionally malicious, such as general distortions, inaccuracies, and misrepresentations of personal histories can be incredibly harmful, meaning this information should be approached through a framework of care and respect for the subjects. In an academic sense, it's also important to note that this information is not necessarily historical fact but rather subjective memories of lived experience, and without the application of thoughtful historical methodologies, could lead to the spread of misinformation or contribute to the aforementioned misuse.
 
Although the corpus is united by a common focus on academic physical sciences and the history of science with a heavy emphasis on the 20th century, the social and historical context of the data varies widely, and spans nearly eight decades of American history. While there is no specific demographic information included outside of potentially the body of the transcript, and despite AIP centering underrepresented histories in its mission and work, it is possibly and likely that many of the same limitations and biases relating to gender identity, race, and class that have existed in western institutions throughout history may also be present in this data. It's also important to remember the text data may not necessarily reflect the entirety of the interview, as subjects are given the opportunity to edit or request parts of their recorded conversation be removed from the text transcript. In total, the thematic focus on academic and technical topics, and the personal, maybe imprecise nature of the information, does suggest that background knowledge in academic science, the individual subject, or the history of science would be helpful in seriously working with this data.
 
With these considerations in mind, the potential uses of this data are vast, and largely enabled by the extensive cataloging and description already performed by AIP staff. Moreover, the capabilities of R and R studio allow for the manipulation and processing of this data in order to yield a number of useful statistical insights and potential scholarly analysis. Several cursory example visualizations are included in this document and will be detailed further to explicate or inspire potential avenues for analysis, and the R scripts used to produce this analysis, and visualizations will be handed to AIP along with the complete dataset.
 
As a general summary of the collection, one can determine the amount of oral history interviews performed by year. A "regular expression" or method for textual pattern matching, was used to identify the four-digit year in the interview date field, and then amended each interview row with its own distinct year column. Basic grouping and counting functions were then used to total the number of interviews that each shared the same year, which can then be easily visualized in R Studio. The results show a consistent pattern of interviews up until around 2020 when the number spikes. This shows a great overview of the project and can provide useful context for the historical and social context of this project, suggesting implications about oral and digital history over time, changes from the pandemic, and even potential staffing or organizational efforts at AIP.

Another overview was created to visualize the most common topics present in the interviews by decade, as a means to look at big picture thematic trends over time. To begin, another regular expression was used in tandem with conditional statements to match and then categorize interview years with the decade they took place in. Similar to specifying the year in the previous analysis, each interview was assigned a decade in order to collocate interviews by a broader level historical abstraction. The subject terms assigned by AIP catalogers were then "unnested" into their own rows, and as each term retained the corresponding information from the source interview, they could again be grouped and counted into the total number of times each subject term was used in each decade. Finally, only the most used subject term for each decade were filtered out in order to produce the graph indicating the most used subject term by decade.

Last, having the entire body text of the interview in the spreadsheet allows for textual analysis, or rather the analysis of the interview by counting the words in the transcript. To begin, the unnest function is used on the entirety of the body text column, giving each word in each transcript its own row. This results in 8,524,169 rows, also indicating the word count of our entire collection of processed transcripts. Because many of these words are not useful or helpful (such as "of," "at," or "the") libraries of code exist in R Studio to remove rows that represent these words, leaving only ones with more specific meaning. From there words may be grouped and counted in a variety of ways.
 
One such way is to calculate a tf-idf score. Standing for "Term Frequency–Inverse Document Frequency" the score is a metric that is assigned to each word in a selected group of text to determine how relevant, or specific it is in its group. The process does so by weighing the number of times the term appears in the selected text in relation to the size of the selection while also accounting for the size of the complete corpus and the word frequency within the larger group as well. Results can be graphed like the example below, and in this case, provides a way of measuring or analyzing what an interview is about by measuring the subject's own words in context. Notably due to the nature of oral history transcripts, often the most common and distinct words in the body text are the last names of the interviewer and interviewee as they are used to indicate who is speaking. Further work with this dataset could see more precise data cleaning to produce a dataset specifically designed for text analysis, or similarly a more sophisticated use of pattern matching code to remove subject last names from the body text.

In total, these summaries and visualizations provide only a small glimpse into potential analysis made possible by this dataset. Data Science and textual analysis are robust fields with strong connections to digital humanities scholarship, and more experienced researchers or practitioners could produce a number of new avenues for research as well as new insights about the oral history collection and its contents. Additionally, the dataset can be used in a more pragmatic or functional way as well, and gaps or absences in metadata or description can be counted or compiled to inform additional cataloging and descriptive work by AIP staff, or used to identify and compile new access points or frameworks from which to design search or navigation features around—such as viewing the data by decade, institution, or location. Finally, these lenses that a data-centered approach can provide afford a foundation for both new editorial content that drives engagement with this collection from a new perspective interest, and that could hopefully or draw in new users or engage researchers in new ways.
